INGENUITY AND ENGINEERING

For the longest time, a career in technology was not for me. I have always been a creative spirit who likes dabbling with watercolors and paints, and I saw STEM fields as being very dull. My prototype image of an engineer was a guy who spent his day hunched over a bright monitor in a dark room doing calculus that I would never understand, and when I thought of STEM I just did not think of me.

During my freshman year of high school, my answer to "What do you want to be?" was "No clue." By my junior year, I said I wanted to be a Graphic Designer. But by my senior year of high school, something changed: I decided to join GET-IT, an organization that sought to introduce girls to technology. Upon joining, I was excited because the first large event of the year was a web design competition. Coincidentally, I had previously taught myself HTML, a language for coding websites, in middle school, and actually really enjoyed it, but never connected something so intriguing and creative like designing a website to working in the "boring" field of tech. GET-IT showed me, however, that technology does not have to be dull and it is a field that welcomes imagination.

I spent months perfecting my Ancestry-themed website and victoriously placed fourth in the Advanced Category, despite it being my first time entering the contest. This really got my creative juices flowing, and I challenged myself to create my own personal self-coded website. I developed the pages, purchased a domain name, uploaded my files and images to a server, and to this day it is still up and running at arielmagyar.com.

Entering Grand Valley, I thought I wanted to be a Mechanical Engineer. I loved to draw and thought that designing parts would be right up my alley. Then in my Intro to Engineering Design I class we started working with Arduinos, programmable prototyping platforms, and I realized that it was the aspect of class I enjoyed the most.

I thought it was incredible that something I programmed could cause a light to flash, a motor to run, or a robot to act according to information from a sensor array. I officially declared my major in Computer Engineering by the end of my freshman year and have been enjoying it ever since.

During winter break of my freshman year of college I wanted to make some extra money so I researched available jobs on campus. I saw a Student Web Developer position was open and applied for it, even with being almost entirely self-taught in HTML and CSS and not having a single clue what queries were. I recently reached my one year anniversary of working in that position on the GVSU Web Team and have developed websites for clients all across the university, ranging from forms for events to full blown systems. I have learned to create tables within the database, draw out information using SQL, and implement it with ColdFusion.

All of that being said, my leading point of pride would have to be finding my passion and taking all steps necessary to pursue it. I am proud to bring creativity to a field that often values scholarship over ingenuity, and I enjoy debunking stereotypes each time I am asked, "What do you want to be?"
